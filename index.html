Evaluating the Code Generation Capabilities of Popular Large Language Models for Front-End Web Development using Neighborhood Pixel Similarity

Dron Datta

Adlai E. Stevenson High School

Sterling Heights, USA

0009-0006-0668-4628


ABSTRACT

The growth of AI and machine learning has led to the initial need for research on which large language model is best capable of replacing front-end web developers based on its performance in the translation of the overall webpage design to code. Being highly complex, trained models, a series of the top six most popular were used in a quantitative, experimental study: ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot, Claude, Pixtral, and LLaMa. However, with a lack of a baseline method in generating a similarity score and the unbalanced sensitivity seen in previous metrics, this study introduces the creation of the Neighborhood-based Pixel Similarity (NbPS) algorithm. By evaluating luminance intensity and local structural similarity with a set tolerance, NbPS states that Claude 3.5-Sonnet is the most capable, with an 84.75% average similarity score across two experiments testing different levels of design complexity and differences between older and newer frameworks that ensure that the results are up to date, such as using React.js, HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. The validity of NbPS was also proven by results that matched trend expectations and similar metrics such as Structural Similarity Index Measure (SSIM) and Mean-Squared Error (MSE).
